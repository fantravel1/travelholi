When is Holi?

Holi falls on the full moon (Purnima) in the Hindu month of Phalguna — usually in late February or March. The festival spans two days: Holika Dahan (bonfire night) and Rangwali Holi (the day of colors).

What to Wear to Holi

The right preparation means more fun and less regret. Here's what experienced Holi travelers recommend.

Wear old, white or light cotton clothes you don't mind permanently staining — white shows colors beautifully
Choose full-coverage clothing — long sleeves and full-length pants protect from harsh colors and sun
Apply coconut oil or mustard oil to all exposed skin and hair before celebrations for easy color removal
Wear closed-toe shoes (old sneakers work perfectly) and bring old sunglasses for eye protection
Skip jewelry, watches, electronics — leave valuables locked in your accommodation
Avoid synthetic fabrics which trap chemical colors against your skin and are harder to clean

Eco-Friendly Holi Colors

Natural colors honor the earth and protect your skin. Here's how to celebrate without causing harm.

Turmeric for vibrant yellow, beetroot for pink and red, henna for green, indigo for blue
Eco-friendly colors are free from heavy metals, lead oxide, and industrial dyes that cause skin damage
Buy certified organic colors from trusted vendors — look for "herbal" or "natural" certification
Use dry colors (gulal) gently rather than throwing them forcefully — apply with care
After celebration, wash with lukewarm water — avoid hot water which opens pores and sets color deeper
Support local artisans who make traditional natural colors from flowers and plant extracts

Lathmar Holi is a centuries-old tradition in Barsana and Nandgaon near Mathura, Uttar Pradesh. Women from Barsana playfully beat men from Nandgaon with long sticks (lathis) while men protect themselves with shields. It's rooted in the legend of Krishna visiting Radha's village and being chased away by the women.

It takes place about a week before the main Holi date and draws huge crowds. It's best for culturally curious travelers who are comfortable with very large, dense crowds and intense energy. Photography should be respectful and consensual.
Plan at least 4–5 days for a proper Holi experience. In Mathura and Vrindavan, celebrations begin up to a week before the official date. Arrive 2–3 days early to acclimate, explore, and attend pre-Holi rituals. The main celebration spans 2 days: Holika Dahan and Rangwali Holi. Allow 1 day after for recovery and travel.

For a deeper experience combining multiple cities (like Barsana + Mathura), plan 7–10 days. Book accommodations well in advance — popular destinations fill up fast, and prices increase significantly during Holi week.
Eco-friendly Holi colors are made from natural ingredients: turmeric (yellow), beetroot (pink/red), henna (green), indigo (blue), and dried flower petals. They're free from heavy metals, lead oxide, and industrial dyes that cause skin irritation and environmental damage.

To use them safely: Buy certified organic colors from trusted vendors. Apply coconut oil to skin and hair before playing. Use dry colors (gulal) gently rather than throwing them forcefully. Avoid getting any powder in eyes or mouth. Wash with lukewarm water afterward — avoid hot water which opens pores. Support local artisans who make traditional natural colors.
Nepal: Called Fagu Purnima, it's a national holiday with vibrant street celebrations in Kathmandu and the Terai. Celebrated one day earlier than in India.

Each diaspora celebration carries its own local flavor while honoring Holi's core meaning of renewal and togetherness. None is "less authentic" than celebrations in India.
Holi symbolizes the triumph of good over evil, rooted in the legend of Prahlad and Holika — where devotion overcomes malice. The Holika Dahan bonfire represents burning away negativity, ego, and past grievances. The color play on Rangwali Holi represents social equality — when covered in color, distinctions of class, caste, age, and gender dissolve.

Holi is a social reset: a time for forgiveness, letting go of old conflicts, and starting fresh. The arrival of spring amplifies this theme of renewal and rebirth. It is also deeply connected to Krishna's playful nature and the love story of Radha and Krishna.
Before Holi: Apply coconut oil, mustard oil, or rich moisturizer on all exposed skin including ears, neck, and nails. Oil your hair thoroughly or cover with a bandana. Wear old sunglasses or clear protective glasses.

During Holi: Use only eco-friendly natural colors. Avoid rubbing color aggressively into anyone's face. Keep your mouth closed during intense play. Stay hydrated.

After Holi: Wash with lukewarm water and mild soap — avoid hot water. Use besan (gram flour) paste mixed with cream to gently remove stubborn color. If color gets in eyes, flush immediately with clean water. Don't scrub harshly — be patient, colors fade within a few days.
Book at least 2–3 months in advance for popular destinations like Mathura, Vrindavan, Jaipur, and Pushkar. For Mathura and Vrindavan specifically, 3–4 months ahead is advisable as options are more limited. Budget guesthouses and homestays sell out even earlier.

Consider staying in nearby cities (like Agra for Mathura trips) if your preferred destination is full. Prices increase significantly during Holi week, so early booking secures better rates. Look for cancellation-friendly policies in case your plans change.
